Lamentations 3:22–23

The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning; great is your faithfulness.
Lamentations • 3 min read • Daily Devotional
Listen
📜

Historical & Biblical Context

Lamentations is one of the most raw and honest books in the Bible. It was written in the immediate aftermath of Jerusalem's destruction by Babylon in 587 BC — the temple burned to the ground, the city walls torn apart, the people marched into exile in chains. The author, likely the prophet Jeremiah, is not writing theology from a comfortable study. He is sitting in rubble. The grief is immediate and total.

Chapter 3 is the emotional center of the book. Verse after verse, the writer describes being "driven into darkness," having his skin wasted away, feeling that God himself has walled him in and shut out his prayers. This is not a cheerful man on an easy day. And then — right in the middle of that darkness — something shifts. "But this I call to mind," he writes, "and therefore I have hope."

What he recalls is stunning: God's steadfast love has not stopped. His mercies — the Hebrew word rachamim, meaning tender compassions, rooted in the same word as "womb" — have not run out. And then the astonishing line: they are new every morning. Not recycled mercy. Fresh mercy. The slate doesn't carry yesterday's weight into today; God brings new compassion with the dawn.

Practical Application for Today

Many of us carry yesterday into today. The guilt of what we said, the failure we haven't forgiven ourselves for, the slow accumulation of regret. We assume God's patience must be wearing thin — that we've come back one too many times. Lamentations, of all books, says the opposite. And it says it from inside suffering, not from comfort. That makes it more credible, not less.

Tomorrow morning, before you check your phone, before the day's demands begin — there is fresh mercy already waiting. It arrived while you slept. You didn't earn it, and you cannot spend yesterday's supply today. This is the logic of grace: it doesn't accumulate debt, it renews. Receive it. Let that change the posture from which you begin.

This is also a verse for people in seasons of real loss. Lamentations doesn't pretend suffering isn't real — it names it without flinching. But it insists that even in devastation, the steadfast love of God is the floor you're standing on, even when you cannot feel it beneath you.

Connection to the Gospel

Jesus is the mercy that is new every morning. He is the fullest expression of hesed — that covenant love that never quits. When he rose from the dead on the first day of the week, at dawn, it was mercy made visible: death had not won, sin had not won, the night was over. Every sunrise since carries that echo.

Paul writes in Romans 8 that nothing — no height, no depth, no principality, nothing present or future — can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus. That love doesn't deplete. It doesn't expire. Christ secured it once and for all, so that what is new every morning is not sentiment but the purchased, guaranteed, everlasting faithfulness of God toward you.

A Closing Prayer

Father, thank you that your mercies don't depend on my consistency. I come to you carrying things I'm not proud of — failures from yesterday, worries about tomorrow. Remind me this morning that your compassion is new, and that it is enough. Let me live from that abundance instead of my own emptiness. Great is your faithfulness. Amen.
