Revelation 21:4

He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away.
Revelation • 3 min read • Daily Devotional
📜

Historical & Biblical Context

Revelation 21 describes the end of all things and the beginning of everything new. The apostle John, exiled on the island of Patmos and writing to churches living under Roman persecution, receives a vision of the new creation: a new heaven, a new earth, the holy city descending like a bride adorned for her husband. And God himself has come to dwell with his people — not in a temple, not mediated by priests, but face to face, fully present. Then, in the most tender image in the entire book: he wipes their tears.

The people reading this letter had lost family members to persecution. They had been stripped of their livelihoods for refusing to bow to the emperor's image. They carried grief that was immediate and ongoing. John's vision is not abstract theology delivered from a distance. It is a direct address to their pain: this is not the end. What you are suffering now will be undone.

"The former things have passed away" is a line worth sitting with. Not forgotten, not minimized — passed away. Done. The Greek word implies not just the ending of old things but the arrival of something categorically new. The universe itself will be renewed, not simply patched. Every loss, every injustice, every disease, every death — it all comes to an end, and something better replaces it permanently.

🌿

Practical Application for Today

Grief is real and it deserves to be honored, not hurried past. This verse doesn't say "it doesn't really hurt that much" or "just keep a good attitude." It says tears exist — and that one day, a hand will wipe them away. That hand belongs to the God of the universe, and the gesture is personal. Not an announcement over a loudspeaker, but a touch. The same God who set the stars in their places will attend to your individual grief.

This verse is not a reason to disengage from the suffering of the present. If anything, it frees us to fully feel it, because we know it doesn't have the final word. We can grieve without tipping into despair. We can sit with friends in their darkest moments without needing to fix everything, because we know the one who actually can fix things is coming.

Let this vision of the future shape how you live today — not as an escape from the present, but as fuel for it. The world being made new starts now, in small ways, whenever we bring comfort to someone who is hurting, restore what's been broken, or work toward what's right. We are participating in a story whose ending is already written.

✝️

Connection to the Gospel

The promise of Revelation 21 is only possible because of what happened on Good Friday and Easter morning. Jesus entered our grief, carried it fully, and defeated its source. On the cross, he absorbed the weight of death, mourning, and pain — and on the third day, he came out the other side, alive. His resurrection body still bore the wounds, but they no longer brought death. That is the trajectory of the whole creation: wounded, yes, but raised.

Every tear God wipes away in the new creation is a tear that Christ has already wept. "Jesus wept" — the shortest verse in the Bible — means our grief was never foreign to God. He walked every road of sorrow we walk, and he walked out the other side. He is not waiting at the finish line with indifference; he is leading us there, scars and all.

A Closing Prayer

Father, I bring you what I'm carrying — the losses, the aches, the things that haven't gone the way I hoped and the grief I'm not sure what to do with. Thank you that you see every tear. Thank you that this is not the final chapter. Give me the courage to fully feel what is real, and the hope to know it won't always be this way. Come, Lord Jesus. Make all things new. Amen.
